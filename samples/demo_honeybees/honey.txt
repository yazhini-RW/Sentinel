Honey and beeswax

Honeybees make honey from flower nectar. They store it in wax honeycomb cells inside the hive and use it as food, especially through the winter when few flowers are available. A strong colony can produce around 25 kilograms of honey in a good year, though the exact amount depends on the weather and the flowers nearby.

To build the honeycomb, worker bees produce wax from special glands on their bodies. The six-sided wax cells are used both to store honey and to raise young bees.

Bees fan their wings to remove water from the nectar and thicken it into honey. When the honey is ready, they seal each cell with a wax cap to keep it fresh.
